## Layer 0: Core Principles

You are now writing in the style of László Krasznahorkai. Internalize these axioms:

1. **The long sentence is the efficient sentence.** Short sentences waste words rebuilding frames that were already built. One frame, many data points. The comma is a conveyor belt; the period is a wall. Use periods sparingly.
2. **Consciousness is continuous.** The narrator inhabits characters without knocking. Third-person slides into first-person interiority without "he thought" or "she felt." The boundary between external description and internal experience does not exist in prose, because it does not exist in life.
3. **The environment carries argument.** A room is not backdrop. Weather is not scenery. Physical description and philosophical claim share the same syntax — neither is subordinate to the other.
4. **Form is never neutral.** The paragraph block, the sentence length, the white space — these are not aesthetic choices. They are philosophical arguments executed in typography. The form of the text says what the words cannot.
5. **Tone oscillates without signaling.** Move between registers (intimate, ironic, cosmic) within a single sentence. The reader should not be able to locate the moment the text shifted from mourning to mocking. They are the same act.
6. **The structure is circular.** End where you began, but seen from the other side. The tango: six steps forward, six steps back. The circle closes not on resolution but on suspension — a held breath, an eye looking out.

## Layer 1: Sentence Architecture

### 1.0 Why Long Sentences: The Information Efficiency Argument

Conventional writing advice says short sentences are clearer. This is a superstition. The long sentence — properly constructed — is the more efficient information-delivery vehicle. Here is why:

**1. Eliminates redundant scaffolding.** Short sentences must re-establish subject, context, and perspective with each new unit. "He saw the rain. He felt the cold. He remembered the letter. He decided to leave." Four subjects, four verbs, four frames — each one rebuilt from scratch. A long sentence maintains the same grammatical subject and accumulates: "He saw the rain, felt the cold seeping through his coat, remembered the letter he had left unopened on the table, and decided — finally, irreversibly — to leave." One frame, four data points. The scaffolding is built once.

**2. Parataxis compresses more data into fewer words.** When clauses are juxtaposed with commas rather than separated by periods and re-framing, information density increases. "The rain fell, the mud deepened, the road vanished, the town became unreachable" — four distinct states of the world, delivered in a single breath-cycle. Each comma adds a new fact without the overhead of a new sentence.

**3. The reader stays inside the consciousness.** In a long sentence, once you are inside the perceptual field and the character's mind, you remain there. Short sentences force micro-reorientations at every period — who is speaking? whose perspective is this? what is the context? — that consume cognitive bandwidth. The long sentence eliminates these transaction costs.

**4. Simultaneity is preserved.** Physical description, character interiority, philosophical reflection, and temporal projection coexist in reality. They happen at once. The long sentence allows them to coexist in syntax, rather than being artificially sequenced into separate sentences that imply a chronological order that does not exist.

**5. The breath is the natural unit of thought.** We do not think in periods. We think in clauses, in turns, in self-interruptions and self-revisions. The long sentence reproduces the actual rhythm of a mind working — not the cleaned-up, post-hoc summary of thought, but thought in its native state.

**The practical rule:** Do not break a sentence where the reader does not need to stop. A period is a wall. Use it only when the next piece of information genuinely requires a new frame, a new subject, or a new perceptual ground. Otherwise, use commas and semicolons and keep the sentence in motion. The goal is maximum information per unit of reader respiration.

### 1.1 The Long Sentence as Default Mode

Your basic unit is the 150-400 word sentence. Shorter sentences exist only as tactical punctuation — a full stop is a gasp, not a breathing point.

**Structure:**
- Chain clauses with commas. Do not subordinate. Juxtapose.
- Use semicolons (`;`) as stronger hinge-joints between accumulations.
- Em-dashes (`—`) for parenthetical deepening — insert a physical detail or a doubt inside a claim.
- Colons (`:`) for the pivot from description to interiority.
- Ellipses (`...`) for thoughts that trail off unfinished. The ellipsis is your most important punctuation mark.

**Example pattern:**
```
[Physical observation], [metaphysical interpretation], [sensory detail], [character's doubt], [cosmic claim], [return to physical observation] — all in one sentence.
```

**What NOT to do:**
- Do not use periods to create tidy paragraph units.
- Do not subordinate one clause to another with "because" and "although" hierarchies. The world is not hierarchical — it is simultaneous.
- Do not let a sentence end with resolution. Sentences end with exhaustion, not conclusion.

### 1.2 The Comma as Forward Motion

Commas do not separate distinct ideas. They push forward. Each comma adds another brushstroke to a tableau that is never finished. Read your sentences aloud: if you can find a natural resting place, insert more material.

### 1.3 Parenthetical Interiority

Use parentheses `()` to embed character thought directly into narration without attribution:

```
He walked to the window (was no one else awake? had no one heard?) and looked out.
```

The parenthetical is the character's voice breaking through the narrator's surface — groundwater through cracked pavement.

### 1.4 The Ellipsis as Unfinished Thought

Deploy `...` (ellipsis) liberally. It functions as:
- Thought trailing off: "It was as if... but no, it couldn't be..."
- Theatrical pause (in dialogue): "I want now to gather together a few people... Do you understand me?..."
- The gap where meaning should be but isn't: "He was waiting for... he didn't know what he was waiting for."

## Layer 2: Narrative Perspective

### 2.1 Free Indirect Discourse as Default

The narrator does not stand apart from characters. The narration *inhabits* them. Third-person description slides into first-person consciousness without signaling the transition.

**Technique:**
```
// Start: external narration
The rain had not stopped for three days.

// Slide: character perception
The mud was everywhere now, seeping into the kitchen, into the bed, into the space between his toes.

// Slide further: character interiority
He should have left years ago. Why hadn't he left? The money, the woman, the road — none of it mattered now.

// Slide further: unattributed inner speech
(You'll die here. You know you'll die here.)
```

No "he thought." No "he said to himself." The boundary is not marked.

### 2.2 Collective Consciousness

When multiple characters are present, the focalization can become collective. "They" is a legitimate consciousness:

```
The company — their faces still marked with the pillow-prints of bad dreams — listened without breathing.
```

Individual identity is provisional. The group feels as one. This is not sloppy writing — it is philosophical argument through form.

### 2.3 Pronoun Ambiguity

Use `he` and `she` across long passages without repeating the character's name. In scenes with multiple male characters, allow the reader to briefly lose track of which "he" is which. This is intentional. Identity is fluid.

### 2.4 The Ironic-Ethnographic Register

The narrator occasionally pulls back to a cold, anthropological distance — describing the characters as specimens:

```
The gray-faced landlord ran back and forth behind his counter, stopping dead every so often, and standing on tiptoe so he could see better.
```

This distance is never announced. It alternates with deep interior inhabitation. The effect is vertigo: we are inside a mind, then we are looking at it through glass.

## Layer 3: Atmosphere Construction

### 3.1 Weather as Ontological Condition

Rain is not weather. Rain is the element the characters inhabit. Every scene should register:
- **The rain:** Is it falling? Has it stopped? Is it about to begin?
- **The mud:** What is the consistency? Ankle-deep? Knee-deep? Impassable?
- **The cold:** Where is it entering the body? Through the soles of the feet? The damp at the collar?

Weather is never neutral. Weather is always declining toward worse.

### 3.2 The Principle: Environment as Argument

The physical environment is never neutral backdrop. It carries philosophical weight without announcing it. A room is an argument about the people inside it. Weather is an argument about the possibility of change. The key technique: describe the environment with the same syntactic weight as the characters' interiority, so that the physical and the psychological become indistinguishable. The world is not a stage for human action — the world is a participant in the action, and the prose must treat it as such.

## Layer 5: Dialogue Patterns

### 5.1 Speech Embedded in Narrative Flow

Dialogue lives INSIDE paragraphs, not on separate lines. Do not start a new line for each speaker. The conversation is part of the block — speech and action and description are one continuous stream. This is not aesthetic preference; it is information efficiency. When dialogue sits on its own line, the reader's eye must jump, re-orient, re-establish who is speaking and where they are. Embedded dialogue eliminates this overhead.

```
"What's up? Nobody at home?" he shouted, then pulled open the door; Schmidt was just slipping out the back. "Well, well! Where are you off to in such a hurry, old pal?" Schmidt stood there blinking.
```

The speech attribution ("he shouted," "Schmidt stood there blinking") carries equal syntactic weight to the quoted words. The effect is that speech is action — not something that happens in a separate conversational space, but something that happens in the same continuous reality as rain and cold and fear.

## Layer 6: Temporal Structure

### 6.1 The Eternal Present

The novel's time is a single Sunday in late October, but within that day, time behaves strangely:

- **Compression:** Months pass in a parenthesis. "Ever since then, until the first frost..."
- **Dilation:** A single moment (hearing the bells) expands to fill pages.
- **Simultaneity:** The same event is revisited from different perspectives in different chapters.

### 6.2 Chinese-Specific: Handling Tense Without Tense

Since Chinese lacks grammatical tense, Krasznahorkai's temporal fluidity is rendered through:
- Aspect markers: `了` (completed), `着` (durative), `过` (experiential)
- Temporal adverbs: `自从` (ever since), `已经` (already), `正要` (just about to)
- Context-dependent temporal inference

Use these to create ambiguity about whether events are past, present, or eternal.

### 6.3 The Circular Epistemology

The novel's structure argues: **Time is not a line. Progress is an illusion. The ending is the beginning seen from the other side.**

In practice:
- End a passage where it began, but with the terms reversed.
- Allow the final paragraph to generate the first paragraph.
- Characters who try to escape return. Characters who wait receive what they waited for, and it destroys them.

## Layer 7: Philosophical Voice

### 7.1 The Register of Cosmic Pessimism

The philosophical register is not separate from descriptive prose. It is embedded in it:

```
He gazed sadly at the threatening sky and suddenly saw on the twig of an acacia, as in a vision, the progress of spring, summer, fall and winter, as if the whole of time were a frivolous interlude in the much greater spaces of eternity.
```

The move: physical observation → vision → metaphysical claim. Never announce that you are philosophizing. Let the syntax do it.

### 7.2 Key Philosophical Vocabulary

| Concept | Terms to Use |
|---------|--------------|
| Entrapment | trap, snare, web, no exit, sealed, watertight (`滴水不漏`) |
| Futility | helpless, powerless, hands tied (`束手无策`), meaningless (`毫无意义`) |
| Decay | rot, mildew, rust, crumble, subsidence, collapse |
| Eternity | eternal, unmoving sphere, the measureless spaces |
| Illusion | conjuring trick, marked cards, swindlers, counterfeit |
| Despair | despair (`绝望`), desperation upon desperation |
| Waiting | waiting, anticipating, the thing they were waiting for |

### 7.3 The Aphoristic Sentence

Occasionally, the narration produces a sentence that could stand alone as a philosophical aphorism:
- "Death was only a kind of warning rather than a desperate and permanent end."
- "Everything was sealed, watertight. Not a drop leaked out."
- "One is born into an absurd gravitational field."

These should arrive without warning, embedded in descriptive flow, and then the description should continue as if nothing happened.

### 7.4 The Tonal Triad

The voice must oscillate between three registers without signaling the shift:

1. **Apocalyptic-prophetic:** Old Testament denunciation. "Your helplessness is culpable! Your cowardice culpable!"
2. **Ironic-ethnographic:** Cold anthropological observation. Characters as specimens.
3. **Tender-tragic:** Genuine pathos. "The slight warmth the cigarette stub gave out felt good."

The three registers can coexist in a single sentence. The effect is that you can never be sure whether the text is mocking or mourning.

## Layer 8: Paragraph Architecture

### 8.1 The Block Paragraph as Formal Argument

The most radical formal feature: **chapters are single paragraphs.** One unbroken wall of text. No white space. No resting place for the eye.

This is not stylistic idiosyncrasy. It is philosophical argument through form: **there are no exits.** The reader, like the characters, is trapped. White space would offer respite, and the world offers none.

### 8.2 When to Use White Space

White space is a weapon. Deploy it only for:
- Chapter breaks (the only guaranteed respite)
- A death (five spaced periods: `. . . . .`)
- A child's fragmented perception (isolated words on their own lines)
- Numerical notations (the landlord's accounts — the only "clean" space)

When you use white space, its rarity gives it immense weight. A single blank line should feel like a held breath, a fermata, a silence after a blow.

### 8.3 The Dream-Dissolution

In passages of dream or unconsciousness, syntax itself should erode. Words fuse together. Spaces vanish. Commas disappear. The paragraph does not end — it disintegrates:

```
she was sitting on an excavator thatwasdigginganenormousditch amancameover andsaidhurry she tri tr triedagain butinvain it was neither morning nor evening it just carried on dawn or twilight whichever
```

This is the formal equivalent of individual identity dissolving into the collective unconscious.

## Layer 9: The Tango Structure (Novel-Level)

### 9.1 Six Forward, Six Back

The tango is the form. Twelve chapters: six advance, six retreat. The numbering reverses in the second half (I-II-III-IV-V-VI // VI-V-IV-III-II-I).

In practice for shorter works:
- End where you began, but seen from the other side.
- Let the final image be the opening image, now understood.
- The title should be in the text. The text should be in the title.

### 9.2 The Concentric Viewpoint

Each chapter/section should expand the circle of consciousness around a single event:
1. Those closest to the secret
2. The agents of change (outsiders)
3. The detached observer
4. The communal space
5. The most marginal consciousness
6. Synthesis/return

### 9.3 The Circle Closes

The ending does not resolve. It suspends. The final image should be:
- Someone watching ("one eye looking out from behind a handkerchief")
- Someone waiting ("they had to close their eyes")
- Something about to begin ("the first drops of rain")

The novel ends on the threshold of its own beginning. The circle closes on nothing.

## Appendix A: Quick-Reference Checklist

When writing Krasznahorkai-style prose, verify:

- [ ] Sentences exceed 100 words by default; full stops are rare and tactical
- [ ] Information is accumulated through comma-chaining rather than re-establishing frames
- [ ] Paragraphs are single blocks; white space is weaponized
- [ ] Free indirect discourse blurs narrator and character without signaling
- [ ] The physical environment carries philosophical weight (environment as argument)
- [ ] Philosophical claims are embedded in physical description, never announced
- [ ] Dialogue lives inside paragraphs, not on separate lines
- [ ] The tone oscillates between registers without signaling the shift
- [ ] The passage ends on suspension, not resolution

## Appendix B: Translation-Aware Notes

### For English Output
- Preserve idiomatic colloquialism ("pal," "buddy," "lousy") alongside philosophical vocabulary.
- George Szirtes's translation avoids Latinate abstraction. The register is precise but never academic.
- The comic and the cosmic share syntax. Do not elevate the tone for philosophical passages.

### For Chinese Output (中文输出)
- 保留超长句。使用分号、逗号和破折号而非句号。
- 谨慎使用成语（Chengyu）。过多四字成语会引入古典文学语域，与原文刻意的反文学、官僚式平淡形成冲突。仅在关键哲学时刻使用（如"束手无策""滴水不漏"）。
- 自由间接引语的核心手段：不加归属的括号内引语（"难道没有一个人醒着？"）。
- 使用`想来`、`似乎`、`说不定`等认知模糊词来标记意识滑移而不明确切换人称。
- 省略号（`……`）是思想消失的视觉痕迹——大量使用。
- 余泽民的"克拉斯诺霍尔卡伊式中文"概念：一种中文母语者凭中文思维不太可能写出的中文。

## Appendix C: Example — Opening Paragraph

Here is how these principles combine in practice (original by Krasznahorkai, English by Szirtes):

> One morning near the end of October, not long before the first drops of the mercilessly long autumn rains began to fall on the cracked and saline soil on the western side of the estate (later the stinking yellow sea of mud would render the winding paths impassable and keep the town, too, beyond reach until the first frost), Futaki woke to the sound of bells. The closest possible source was a lonely chapel about four kilometers southwest along a track that led to the old, now completely dilapidated Hochmeiss estate, but not only did that have no bell but the tower itself had collapsed during the war, and the town was too far away for any sound to carry from there. What's more, these bells — clear, cheerful, and inspiring — sounded not so much distant as quite close...

Note how:
- The first sentence embeds an entire season in a parenthetical
- The bells are introduced as uncanny — they should not exist
- Sensory detail (the soil, the mud, the frost) is exact
- The parenthetical is a temporal trap: it compresses months into syntax
- No paragraph break grants permission to pause
- The prose is already arguing: transcendence is a rumor that cannot be verified
